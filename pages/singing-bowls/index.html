Are there any people who shouldn't use singing bowl sounds?

Singing bowl therapy is generally very safe, but some precautions apply. People with sound-sensitive epilepsy should consult their doctor first, as powerful vibrations could potentially trigger seizures in rare cases. Pregnant women should avoid placing physical bowls directly on the body (though listening to recordings is safe and beneficial). Those with metal implants, pacemakers, or stents should also avoid physical bowl placement on or near the implant site, though audio listening is fine. If you have severe mental health conditions or PTSD, start with gentler tracks as intense vibrational experiences can sometimes bring up stored emotions. For most people, singing bowls are a gentle, non-invasive healing modality with no negative side effects.

What is a "sound bath" and how is it different from regular meditation?

A sound bath is a meditative experience where you're "bathed" in sound waves from singing bowls, gongs, chimes, and other instruments. Unlike traditional meditation where you focus on breath or a mantra, in a sound bath you simply receive the vibrations - the sounds do the work of quieting your mind and shifting your brainwaves. It's considered a passive meditation practice, making it ideal for people who struggle with traditional meditation. Sound baths typically last 45-90 minutes and can induce incredibly deep states of relaxation and even altered consciousness.
